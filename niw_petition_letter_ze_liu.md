III. SUBSTANTIAL MERIT AND NATIONAL IMPORTANCE
A. SUBSTANTIAL MERIT OF THE PROPOSED ENDEAVOR

The proposed endeavor to advance the quality, consistency, and effectiveness of indigent criminal defense services in the United States has substantial merit because it directly strengthens constitutional protections, improves the accuracy and fairness of criminal adjudication, and enhances the operational integrity of the U.S. justice system. Indigent defense is not limited to individual legal representation; it functions as a structural mechanism through which core constitutional guarantees are implemented in practice across the criminal justice system.
1. Constitutional Value: Safeguarding Fundamental Legal Protections
The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel in criminal prosecutions, forming a foundational safeguard of the American justice system. In Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335 (1963) (Exhibit 1), the U.S. Supreme Court held that legal representation is an essential requirement of a fair trial, emphasizing that justice cannot depend on a defendant’s financial capacity. Subsequent jurisprudence has extended this protection to ensure meaningful implementation across all stages of criminal proceedings.
However, the constitutional guarantee is not self-executing. Its practical realization depends on the capacity of indigent defense systems to provide effective representation. As recognized by the Court in Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984), (Exhibit 2) the constitutional standard is violated when counsel’s performance undermines the reliability of proceedings. This establishes that the right to counsel is meaningful only when defense representation is competent, adequately resourced, and capable of adversarial testing.
Accordingly, strengthening indigent defense services directly reinforces the enforcement of a core constitutional guarantee, ensuring that procedural rights are not merely formal but functionally effective.
2. Judicial Value: Improving Accuracy and Reliability of Criminal Adjudication
A central judicial value of indigent defense lies in its role in improving the accuracy, fairness, and reliability of criminal adjudication. The effectiveness of the adversarial system depends on whether defense counsel can meaningfully test the prosecution’s case across all stages of litigation.
(a) Preventing Wrongful Convictions
Wrongful convictions represent a critical failure of judicial accuracy. Empirical research has consistently identified inadequate defense investigation, limited resources, and high caseloads as contributing factors in wrongful conviction cases. The National Registry of Exonerations has documented that ineffective or insufficient legal defense is a recurring factor in a substantial proportion of exoneration cases in the United States (National Registry of Exonerations, University of Michigan Law School, 2024). (Exhibit 3)

These findings demonstrate that defense quality is directly linked to adjudicative accuracy. By ensuring effective case preparation, evidentiary review, and adversarial testing, indigent defense reduces the risk of erroneous convictions and strengthens confidence in judicial outcomes.
(b) Ensuring Fair and Informed Plea Bargaining
The majority of criminal convictions in the United States are obtained through plea bargaining rather than trial adjudication, making plea negotiations a central component of judicial decision-making (U.S. Department of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023).(Exhibit 4) The Supreme Court has recognized that plea bargaining must meet constitutional standards of effective assistance of counsel.
In Missouri v. Frye, 566 U.S. 134 (2012), (Exhibit 5)and Lafler v. Cooper, 566 U.S. 156 (2012), (Exhibit 6) the Court held that ineffective legal representation during plea negotiations can produce constitutionally deficient outcomes. These decisions underscore that plea bargaining is not merely an administrative process but a critical adjudicative stage requiring competent legal advocacy.
Effective indigent defense ensures that plea decisions are informed, voluntary, and based on accurate legal and evidentiary assessment, thereby improving both fairness and reliability of outcomes.
(c) Ensuring Full Evidence Disclosure and Reliable Adjudication
Reliable adjudication depends on complete and transparent evidentiary disclosure. In Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963) (Exhibit 7), the Supreme Court held that suppression of materially favorable evidence violates due process. This obligation was further reinforced in Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U.S. 419 (1995), (Exhibit 8), which emphasized the prosecution’s duty to disclose material evidence and the importance of adversarial testing in ensuring accuracy.
Indigent defense counsel plays a critical role in operationalizing these constitutional protections by identifying disclosure gaps, pursuing discovery, and challenging incomplete evidentiary production. These functions ensure that judicial determinations are based on a complete and reliable evidentiary record.
3. Institutional Value: Effective Administration of Justice
Beyond individual case outcomes, indigent defense carries substantial institutional value by improving the efficiency and sustainability of the criminal justice system. Its systemic impact is reflected in three interrelated mechanisms: early case assessment, improved case resolution, and reduced unnecessary litigation.
(a) Efficient Courts Through Early Identification of Key Issues
Effective indigent defense enhances judicial efficiency by enabling early identification of legal and factual issues. Defense counsel is often the first actor to evaluate evidentiary strength and constitutional concerns after charges are filed, allowing courts to differentiate between cases requiring full litigation and those suitable for early resolution.
Early defense involvement has been associated with reduced case processing time and lower pretrial detention durations (National Legal Aid & Defender Association, 2020). (Exhibit 9). This improves judicial triage, reduces backlog, and enhances the efficiency of court operations.
(b) Better Case Resolution Through Informed Decision-Making
Given that most criminal cases are resolved through plea agreements, the quality of case resolution depends heavily on the effectiveness of defense counsel. The Supreme Court has emphasized that competent legal representation is essential to constitutionally valid plea outcomes (Lafler v. Cooper, 566 U.S. 156 (2012), (Exhibit 6); Missouri v. Frye, 566 U.S. 134 (2012). (Exhibit 5)
Indigent defense improves case resolution by ensuring that decisions are based on accurate assessment of evidence, legal exposure, and sentencing consequences. This reduces uninformed or coerced pleas while improving consistency and predictability in case outcomes.
(c) Reducing Unnecessary Litigation and Judicial Burden
Effective indigent defense also reduces unnecessary litigation by resolving weak or legally insufficient cases at earlier stages through motions practice, negotiation, or dismissal. This reduces trial volume and conserves judicial resources for cases requiring full adjudication.
The National Center for State Courts has identified case overload and trial delay as persistent structural challenges in state criminal systems, emphasizing the importance of early resolution mechanisms in maintaining efficiency (National Center for State Courts, 2021). (Exhibit 10). Indigent defense contributes directly to this objective by improving early case screening and reducing downstream litigation costs.
CONCLUSION
Collectively, the proposed endeavor demonstrates substantial merit because it operates at the intersection of constitutional enforcement, judicial accuracy, and institutional efficiency. By strengthening indigent defense services, the endeavor directly supports the effective implementation of Sixth Amendment protections, enhances the reliability of criminal adjudication through improved defense participation, and contributes to a more efficient and sustainable justice system. Rather than serving only individual defendants, the proposed endeavor functions as a structural component of the U.S. criminal justice system, reinforcing its fairness, accuracy, and operational integrity at a national level.
B. NATIONAL IMPORTANCE OF THE PROPOSED ENDEAVOR 

Unlike the substantial merit inquiry, which examines the intrinsic value of the proposed endeavor, the national importance inquiry focuses on whether the endeavor has broader implications that extend beyond the interests of individual clients or a single employer. Matter of Dhanasar makes clear that an endeavor may possess national importance when its prospective impact reaches beyond a localized context and contributes to a field or issue of national significance.
Ms. Liu's proposed endeavor satisfies this standard. By advancing the quality, consistency, and effectiveness of indigent criminal defense services through high-quality representation and the development of effective, replicable defense practices, her endeavor addresses a nationwide constitutional challenge, generates improvements capable of implementation across public defense systems, and advances significant public interests underlying the American criminal justice system.
1. The Proposed Endeavor Addresses a Nationwide Constitutional Challenge
The proposed endeavor possesses national importance because it addresses systemic challenges affecting indigent criminal defense throughout the United States rather than the operational needs of any single public defender office. Across the country, public defense systems face persistent shortages of qualified attorneys, excessive caseloads, and substantial disparities in access to legal representation, particularly in rural and underserved communities. These challenges have been widely recognized by the American Bar Association, the National Legal Aid & Defender Association, and the Sixth Amendment Center as nationwide structural deficiencies that threaten the effective delivery of constitutionally required legal services (Exhibits 11–13).
“The Oregon supreme court has ruled that a large number of criminal cases across the state must be dismissed due to a severe shortage of public defenders, a major decision that attorneys say will impact more than 1,400 pending cases.
The problem has been years in the making and has become a significant constitutional crisis, as people charged with crimes are routinely unable to fight their cases as they wait weeks, months or sometimes years for the state to appoint them lawyers. The attorney shortage – due in part to the increasing difficulty of recruiting attorneys for the low-salary, high-caseload jobs – has meant that people have had cases hanging over them for extended periods of time, impacting their housing, employment and families, advocates say.” (Exhibit 14)

Similar constitutional deficiencies have been documented in numerous states, including Oregon, Missouri, Louisiana, New York, and Washington. The consequences of this deficiency are profound. Without effective legal representation, defendants face increased risks of wrongful convictions, prolonged detention, and significant collateral consequences affecting employment, housing, and family stability. In extreme instances, courts have been compelled to dismiss criminal cases due to the state’s failure to provide counsel, highlighting the constitutional magnitude of the issue.
The shortage of public defenders is not simply an employment issue, it is a constitutional concern. Since Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335 (1963) , (Exhibit 1), every state has been constitutionally obligated to provide effective legal representation to indigent criminal defendants. As a result, deficiencies in public defense systems implicate a nationwide constitutional obligation rather than isolated local administrative concerns. The American Bar Association has repeatedly concluded that excessive workloads, insufficient funding, and attorney shortages undermine compliance with the Sixth Amendment and impair the effective administration of justice (Exhibit 2: ABA Standing Committee on Legal Aid and Indigent Defense, Ten Principles of a Public Defense Delivery System).
These challenges are particularly acute in rural jurisdictions, where attorney shortages frequently leave indigent defendants without timely access to constitutionally adequate representation. The Legal Service Corporation has documented significant disparities in public defense capacity among states and counties, demonstrating that unequal access to effective counsel remains a persistent national issue rather than a localized phenomenon (Exhibit 15).
Existing shortages cannot be resolved solely through increasing attorney numbers. Improving the quality, consistency, and efficiency of indigent defense practices is equally important to maximizing limited public defense resources. Ms. Liu's proposed endeavor directly advances this objective by developing and promoting effective, replicable practices capable of improving representation across similarly situated public defender offices. Accordingly, Ms. Liu's proposed endeavor addresses a structural constitutional challenge shared by public defense systems nationwide. Its significance therefore extends well beyond the immediate interests of a single employer or jurisdiction.
