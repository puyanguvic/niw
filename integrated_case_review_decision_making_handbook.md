# Part I — Governance and Operating Standards

## 1. Purpose and Operating Principles

This framework is designed for a recurring problem in high-volume defense
practice: a decisive fact, deadline, conflict, missing item, legal issue, or
client consequence may exist in the file but surface too late to affect the
case. The framework places review points at the moments when information is most
likely to be lost and connects every identified issue to a responsible person,
next action, and due date.

The framework has five operating principles:

1. **Review by decision, not only by court date.** A case moves through defined
   decision gates, each with an identifiable output.
2. **Convert information into action.** A risk flag or issue is incomplete until
   it has an owner, a next step, and a due date.
3. **Test the record in two directions.** Reconstruct what happened over time,
   then test the admissible proof against every element of every count.
4. **Keep the client at the center.** Legal exposure, collateral consequences,
   custody pressure, communication needs, and the client's objectives must be
   considered together.
5. **Preserve lessons as process.** Reusable lessons must be submitted through
   the controlled revision process so that an approved improvement can revise a
   prompt, checklist, or template rather than remain only in an individual
   lawyer's memory.

### 1.1 Continuous controls and staged decision points

The gate numbers organize the file; they do not create a strictly linear
sequence or permit a time-sensitive issue to wait. Conflict, urgency, deadlines,
client communication, confidentiality, capacity, and related-matter review are
continuous controls. They must be reassessed whenever their trigger occurs.
Several gates may remain open at once, and a completed gate must be reopened
when new information materially changes the prior review.

| Gate | Decision question | Required work product | Exit condition |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Conflict clearance | May counsel proceed without compromising loyalty or confidentiality? | Conflict-clearance record | Cleared or escalated; recheck triggers recorded |
| 2. Urgent-risk screening | What cannot safely wait? | Priority and consequence flags | Immediate actions assigned and calendared |
| 3. Client and case mapping | What happened, what matters to the client, and what controls the schedule? | Case map, client chronology, objectives record | Known facts, deadlines, participants, and objectives mapped |
| 4. Discovery control | What is referenced, received, accessible, reviewed, or missing? | Discovery ledger and deficiency log | Material reconciled or follow-up/escalation pending with dates |
| 5. Merits and legal review | What can the State prove with admissible evidence, and what legal issues require action? | Elements-to-proof matrix, event timeline, issue worksheet | Proof gaps and legal issues converted into tasks |
| 6. Resolution analysis | What are the realistic options and consequences for this client? | Plea/outcome comparison and negotiation plan | Client receives sufficient advice and makes any decision allocated to the client |
| 7. Hearing preparation | What relief is sought, what proof is needed, and what record must be made? | Hearing plan | Primary and fallback routes prepared |
| 8. Trial preparation | If resolution does not protect the client's interests, is the case trial-ready? | Trial plan, witness/exhibit materials, instruction and preservation plan | Trial readiness confirmed or deficiencies assigned |
| 9. Feedback and refinement | What should occur earlier or more clearly next time? | Post-matter review and revision entry | Lesson incorporated, assigned, or consciously declined |

“Reviewed—current as of” means that the minimum review required at that stage was documented
as of a stated date. It does not mean that discovery is closed, the legal
analysis cannot change, or the matter is safe from later developments. The
assigned lawyer must identify the event that will trigger the next review.

Do not mark a gate “Reviewed—current as of” if a required material task has no
accepted owner, an applicable deadline is not in the approved calendar, or a
material overdue item lacks a documented escalation and protective plan. Only
the lawyer responsible for the decision gate, or a supervisor who expressly
assumes that responsibility, may approve its reviewed-current status.

### 1.2 File status vocabulary

Use consistent status labels so that a new upload or a crowded calendar does not
hide unfinished work.

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Not started** | Required review has not begun |
| **Open** | Issue or task identified; action remains |
| **Waiting—external** | A specific request has been made; follow-up date is set |
| **Waiting—client** | Client input is needed; communication plan is set |
| **Escalated** | Supervisor, specialist, court, or other authorized reviewer must act |
| **Overdue—escalated** | A due item remains unresolved; the risk, protective plan, and escalation are recorded |
| **Reviewed—current as of** | The required review was performed on the information available at the recorded date and time; later triggers may reopen it |
| **Resolved** | A discrete task or issue is finished and the result is recorded |
| **Not applicable—reason recorded** | Attorney has made and recorded a case-specific determination that the item does not apply |

“Received” is not the same as “reviewed.” “Referenced” is not the same as
“received.” “Uploaded” is not the same as “complete and accessible.”

### 1.3 Scope and application

Upon adoption, this handbook applies to every North County Public Defense lawyer
handling an adult criminal matter in Washington superior court or a court of
limited jurisdiction. It also applies, to the extent assigned by a supervisor,
to Rule 9 legal interns and to lawyers providing limited, calendar, conflict, or
contract coverage for the office. Nonlawyer team members must follow the
portions assigned to their role, but no workflow assignment transfers the
lawyer's responsibility for legal judgment, supervision, or client advice.

Use of this handbook in juvenile, appellate, civil-commitment, family-defense,
or other specialized matters requires a practice-area supplement approved by
the Managing Attorney. A checklist designed for one case type must not be
treated as complete for a different case type.

The framework applies from the earliest of assignment, appointment, or
substantive client contact through authorized withdrawal, substitution, or file
closure. Limited-scope and emergency representations require a proportionate
record of the scope, work performed, advice given, outstanding issues, and
handoff.

| Term | Meaning in this handbook |

| **Should** | The expected practice; a material departure should be based on case-specific professional judgment and documented when necessary to protect continuity or permit review |
| **May** | A permitted option requiring case-specific judgment |

| **Promptly** | As soon as reasonably practicable and no later than the next business day, unless a deadline, custody, safety, evidence-loss, or other risk requires earlier action |

Checking a box records completion of a process; it does not establish that the
underlying legal or factual conclusion is correct. Entries must be sufficiently
specific to permit the responsible lawyer—and, when authorized, a covering or
supervising lawyer—to understand the issue, status, and next action.

### 1.5 Governing authority and conflict rule

This handbook must be read consistently with controlling law and professional
obligations. It is not a substitute for legal research. Lawyers must verify
current authority as of the date of the relevant advice, filing, waiver,
hearing, or trial.

For policy maintenance, sources are generally classified as follows:

1. controlling federal and Washington constitutions, statutes, decisions,
   court rules, case-specific orders, and official local requirements;
2. the Washington Rules of Professional Conduct and Court-adopted Standards for
   Indigent Defense;
3. binding appointment, contract, and office requirements that are consistent
   with higher authority and professional independence;
4. this handbook and its approved local and practice-area addenda; and
5. WSBA, ABA, and other approved performance guidance, which must not be
   described as independently binding unless adopted by controlling authority.

This classification is not a legal opinion about priority in a particular
conflict. The responsible lawyer must research the actual authorities and seek
review when their interaction is uncertain. Decisions allocated to the client
by law and professional rules remain the client's decisions regardless of an
internal preference or workflow.

When requirements appear to conflict, the lawyer must:

1. protect any immediate deadline, liberty, confidentiality, safety, or
   preservation interest that can be protected lawfully;
2. identify the controlling constitution, statute, rule, court order,
   professional obligation, or client decision;
3. consult current authority rather than relying on the handbook's summary;
4. escalate the issue promptly if it cannot be resolved with confidence; and
5. document the resolution and any resulting change to the case plan.

No manager, contract term, performance measure, checklist, or workflow may
direct a lawyer to compromise professional independence or a client's lawful
interests. No handbook provision authorizes disclosure of client information,
contact with a represented person, waiver of a client's right, or action
allocated to the client by law or professional rule.

### 1.6 Roles and accountability

| Role | Minimum responsibilities under this handbook |
|---|---|
| **Assigned lawyer / lead counsel** | Owns the legal analysis, client relationship, gate completion, task delegation, deadline control, advice, and accuracy of the case record |
| **Covering lawyer** | Reviews the available control page and scope of coverage; addresses the appearance competently; records material events, advice, offers, rulings, and follow-up before handoff |
| **Supervising lawyer** | Provides accessible consultation; reviews escalated risks; confirms appropriate supervision and training; acts on workload and quality concerns; does not replace the assigned lawyer's professional independence |
| **Rule 9 intern / subordinate lawyer** | Works within authorization, training, and supervision; identifies uncertainty; does not treat supervisory direction as eliminating personal professional obligations |
| **Investigator / mitigation specialist / social-service professional** | Performs assigned work within role and confidentiality requirements; documents sources and results; promptly reports material developments |
| **Paralegal / legal assistant** | Supports calendaring, file control, discovery processing, communication routing, and authorized drafting; immediately elevates discrepancies and deadlines; does not give legal advice |
| **Managing Attorney / Executive Director** | Owns the policy; maintains systems reasonably designed to support compliant practice; designates reviewers; addresses systemic workload, training, and resource issues |
| **Policy administrator** | Maintains the controlled copy, forms, revision log, training record, annual review, and source-verification schedule |
| **Information-security / records lead** | Approves systems and handling standards; receives and coordinates response to security, privacy, access, and records incidents |

Delegation must state the task, purpose, deadline, reporting method, and any
confidentiality or contact limitation. The assigning lawyer must provide
appropriate instruction, remain available for questions, review work in
proportion to risk, and confirm completion. Silence or lack of a status update is
not proof that a delegated task is complete.

### 1.7 Universal minimum practice standards

The following controls apply to every matter, with depth proportionate to the
matter's complexity and consequences:

- **Conflict control:** complete initial review before substantive work when
  practicable and renew it when new participants or related matters appear.
- **Urgency control:** identify what cannot safely await the next ordinary
  meeting or court date.
- **Deadline control:** independently identify, calculate, verify, and calendar
  deadlines; do not rely solely on a docket, another participant, or an
  automatically generated date.
- **Client communication:** establish a workable communication plan, respond or
  acknowledge within a reasonable time, and provide information sufficient for
  informed participation in decisions.
- **Factual and legal review:** investigate and analyze the facts, elements,
  admissibility, defenses, procedures, consequences, and realistic options
  before consequential advice.
- **Discovery control:** distinguish referenced, requested, received,
  accessible, reviewed, corrected, supplemental, and outstanding material.
- **Client authority:** accurately identify and preserve decisions allocated to
  the client; record the advice and decision without substituting counsel's
  preference.
- **Preparation and preservation:** prepare for each consequential appearance
  and identify the record needed to preserve material issues.
- **Continuity:** maintain a file from which an authorized covering lawyer can
  identify the posture, next event, urgent issue, and required action.
- **Escalation:** raise conflicts, capacity constraints, missed or threatened
  deadlines, unfamiliar high-consequence issues, and security incidents early
  enough for corrective action.

This framework is a floor, not a ceiling. Completion may require investigation,
expert or specialist assistance, co-counsel, additional client meetings, legal
research, or a case-specific work product not listed here.

### 1.8 Workload and capacity protection

The framework must not be used to imply that checklists can cure an excessive
workload. The assigned lawyer must continuously assess whether the lawyer has
the time, support, competence, and resources necessary to provide quality
representation in each existing matter and to accept additional assignments.
The assessment must account for open matters, case complexity, custody, trials,
partial and calendar representations, supervision, leave, training,
administrative work, and other professional commitments—not only the number of
new cases assigned.

When capacity may be inadequate, the lawyer must promptly provide the
supervisor with enough information to permit action, including the affected
deadlines, detained clients, trials, high-consequence matters, unreviewed
discovery, and requested support. Available responses may include reprioritizing
nonmaterial work, assigning support, obtaining co-counsel or specialist help,
reallocating matters, reducing or stopping new assignments, or seeking other
relief consistent with governing law and office authority.

A supervisor receiving a good-faith capacity report must assess the risk,
identify immediate protections, document the response, and elevate any systemic
resource or assignment problem to the Managing Attorney. The individual lawyer
must continue to protect existing clients and may not assume that reporting the
problem alone resolves the professional obligation.

Each lawyer and supervisor must use the office's approved workload record. It
must be capable of showing, as applicable:

| Workload control | Current record / source |
|---|---|
| Annual assignments and case credits by case type | |
| Open matters and complexity / weighting adjustments | |
| Custody cases, trials, contested hearings, and hard deadlines | |
| Calendar, partial, specialty-court, and other limited representations | |
| Mixed public-defense / private-practice percentage, if applicable | |
| Supervision, administration, training, leave, and other noncase time | |
| Experience-based assignment limitation or enhanced supervision | |

Do not hard-code a caseload ceiling from memory or assume that a final numerical
standard, a local weighting method, and a phased implementation requirement are
the same. Verify the current Court Standards, implementation orders,
certification requirements, and the local written and published policy recorded
in Appendix E and the applicable addendum.

### 1.9 Confidentiality, records, and technology

All case records must be created, stored, transmitted, accessed, and disposed of
only through approved systems and in accordance with the office's security,
records-retention, protective-order, and litigation-hold requirements.

Lawyers and staff must:

- use access controls and approved methods appropriate to the sensitivity of the
  material;
- verify recipients, attachments, permissions, and redactions before sending or
  sharing information;
- use the minimum client information reasonably necessary for an authorized
  operational purpose;
- maintain source identity, date received, version, and review status for
  discovery and other material evidence;
- avoid placing substantive case information only in personal notes, personal
  email, unapproved messaging, or an unapproved device or storage location;
- comply with protective orders, discovery-use restrictions, redaction duties,
  and limitations on client access to restricted material; and
- report suspected loss, misdirection, unauthorized access, alteration,
  disclosure, malware, or system compromise immediately through the approved
  incident channel.

Client information may not be entered into a public or unapproved generative-AI
system, transcription service, translation service, cloud repository, research
platform, or other third-party tool. An approved tool may be used only for its
approved purpose, with required settings and human review. The responsible
lawyer must verify citations, quotations, factual assertions, calculations,
deadlines, and legal analysis produced or assisted by technology.

If an incident occurs, do not conceal it, delete relevant records, or attempt an
unapproved technical remedy. Preserve relevant information, take any authorized
immediate containment step, notify the designated security and supervisory
contacts, and protect any associated court or client deadline.

### 1.10 Authorized deviations and emergencies

A lawyer may adapt the order, depth, or format of a workflow when case-specific
professional judgment reasonably requires it. A deviation does not excuse an
underlying legal, ethical, court-ordered, or client obligation. When a material
required control cannot be completed before a decision or event, the lawyer
must record:

- what remains incomplete and why;
- the risk created by proceeding;
- what immediate protective action was taken;
- what the client was told, when material to the client's decision;
- whether supervision or specialist advice was obtained; and
- the owner and date for completing or reassessing the item.

In a genuine emergency, the lawyer must act within the lawyer's competence to
protect the client's immediate interests, limit the action to what the
circumstances reasonably require, consult or escalate as soon as practicable,
and complete the record after the emergency is controlled.

### 1.11 Training, supervision, and acknowledgment

Before independent use, each lawyer must receive orientation on the decision
gates, approved systems, calendaring, conflict escalation, confidentiality,
client communication, workload reporting, and the practice supplements relevant
to the lawyer's assignments. Supervisors must provide closer review when a
lawyer is new to practice, new to a case type, or handling a matter whose
complexity exceeds prior experience.

Initial implementation must include a supervised sample matter or simulation
and documented review of the lawyer's ability to locate current sources, use the
approved systems, identify escalation triggers, and explain a consequential
decision. New-lawyer and new-practice-area supervision plans must state review
milestones, the work requiring advance approval or observation, and the criteria
for expanding independent responsibility. Material changes to law, systems, or
forms require targeted change training before affected personnel rely on the
new process.

The policy administrator must maintain:

- the current controlled handbook and forms;
- onboarding and periodic refresher training materials;
- records of issuance, training, and acknowledgment;
- a channel for questions and improvement proposals;
- a schedule for reviewing legal sources and local-rule supplements; and
- a record of approved revisions and retired versions.

Acknowledgment confirms receipt and responsibility to use the handbook; it does
not certify that a lawyer has mastered every subject or waive the lawyer's right
or duty to request training, supervision, resources, or workload relief.

**Responsible role:** Assigned lawyer; administrative fields may be delegated

**Storage:** Approved client file / case-management system

Open the case-control record at assignment and update it whenever a material

record is an orientation and decision-control tool. It does not replace the
official conflict system, docket, court record, case-management system, or

### 2.0 Assignment acceptance and file opening

Before or promptly after accepting an assignment, the assigned lawyer must
confirm:

- the identity of the client, court, cause number, appointment or assigned
  scope, and source and time of assignment;
- the lawyer's qualification and authorization for the case type, including any

- immediate conflicts and the need for an emergency limited review while a
  conflict question is resolved;

  preservation or safety issue;
- whether another lawyer has appeared or retains responsibility for any portion
  of the matter;
- that the matter has been opened in each required approved system; and
- that responsibility for the next client contact and court appearance is
  unambiguous.

If the assignment is incomplete, duplicated, outside the lawyer's authority, or
inconsistent with capacity or qualification requirements, the lawyer must
protect any immediate interest and escalate before assuming that another person
will correct it.

## 3. Gate 1 — Initial and Continuing Conflict Review

**Storage:** Official conflict system; the client file should contain only the

### 3.1 Initial search set

Search all names and relationships required by office policy, including, as
applicable:

- client name, known aliases, and material name variations;
- alleged victim or complainant;
- co-defendants and jointly investigated persons;
- principal witnesses and other material participants;
- related cause numbers and substantially related matters; and
- persons or entities newly identified through interviews or discovery.

| Name / identifier | Role | Search date | Possible match | Risk type | Result / reviewer |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | | |

Risk types may include direct adversity, former-client confidentiality, current
or former co-defendant adversity, and a connection to another pending or
substantially related matter. A possible match must be handled under governing
ethical rules and office policy; the checklist itself does not resolve the
conflict.

A database search is issue spotting, not a legal clearance. The authorized
reviewer must analyze the duties implicated, including current-client,
former-client, and imputation questions; whether the relevant public-defense
entities or teams are treated as one firm for the issue; whether the conflict is
consentable; what informed consent and written confirmation would require; and
whether screening is legally available and operationally sufficient. Management
approval cannot cure a nonconsentable conflict, and a screen may be used only
when governing authority permits it. Record only the minimum authorized status
in the ordinary client file; information concerning another client remains in
the restricted conflict system.

- supplemental police report;
- body-worn camera, interview, dispatch, or other recording;
- client or witness interview;
- amended charging document;
- new co-defendant, related case, or cross-jurisdiction information; or
- prosecutor, court, investigator, or service-provider communication.

### 7.3 Material-specific review prompts

#### Recordings

- Is the complete file produced, playable, and preserved in an approved format?

- Does the recording actually support the attributed conduct or statement?

- What opportunity did the declarant have to perceive and remember?

#### Photographs, physical evidence, and testing

- What does the item actually depict, measure, or establish?
- Who can authenticate it and establish the necessary foundation?
- Are collection, condition, chain of custody, methodology, or calibration
  adequately documented?
- Which element does it support, and what does it not establish?

### 12.3 Refinement test

Before adopting a change, ask:

- Does the change move a material issue earlier in the case?
- Does it connect the issue to a concrete action and responsible person?
- Does it protect individualized judgment rather than replace it?
- Is it usable under high-volume conditions?
- Does it comply with current law, ethics, office policy, confidentiality, and
  data-security requirements?
- Can another trained lawyer understand when and how to use it?
- Is the benefit worth the time required to maintain it?

Examples of appropriate refinements include adding source-by-source discovery
reconciliation after a missing digital item, adding a foundation and contingency
column after an evidentiary ruling changes witness order, or requiring verified
treatment and transportation logistics before a release request is filed.

## 13. Supervisory and Specialist Escalation

Escalation is a required protective process, not an admission of error and not a
transfer of responsibility. Unless responsibility is expressly reassigned and
accepted, the lawyer of record must continue taking lawful steps within the
lawyer's authority to protect the client.

### 13.1 Required escalation events

Notify the direct supervisor and the designated subject-matter contact
immediately for a critical event and promptly for every other event below:

- an actual or possible conflict, disqualification, confidentiality exposure,
  or unauthorized access to conflict information;
- a missed, threatened, disputed, or not reliably calculable filing, trial,
  offer, notice, appeal, preservation, or other material deadline;
- failure to communicate an offer or inability to obtain a client decision
  before an offer or right may expire;
- inability to prepare competently for an imminent hearing or trial;
- workload, fatigue, illness, leave, departure, qualification, or training
  limitation that threatens competent and diligent representation or leaves a
  matter without clear responsibility;
- a competency concern, serious communication barrier, or unavailable necessary
  interpreter or accommodation affecting a consequential event;
- potential immigration consequences or another unfamiliar mandatory or severe
  consequence requiring individualized specialist analysis;
- material missing, inaccessible, lost, altered, or at-risk evidence, including
  a deficiency that may require preservation action or court relief;
- a possible breach of duties concerning candor, disclosure, contact,
  safekeeping, client direction, or another professional obligation;
- an unauthorized disclosure, misdirected communication, lost device,
  suspicious access, malware, or other privacy or information-security event;
- an immediate liberty, safety, medical, housing, family, or irreversible-right
  risk that ordinary scheduling will not protect;
- a sanction, serious client complaint, material adverse event, or circumstance
  potentially requiring advice from ethics counsel, coverage counsel, an
  insurer, or another authorized responder; or
- a decision or task outside the person's authority, assigned role, or current
  competence.

Pending review of a possible conflict, substantive work and access must stop
except to the limited extent reasonably necessary and authorized to protect an
imminent client right. Detailed match information remains in the restricted
conflict system; the ordinary client file contains only the authorized
clearance reference, date, and disposition.

### 13.2 Escalation levels and routing

| Level | Definition | Required action |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical—act now** | Delay may cause loss of liberty, safety, confidentiality, evidence, a deadline, or a client option | Take any lawful immediate protective step; contact the supervisor and designated lead at once; if acknowledgment would come too late, proceed to the next contact |
| **Urgent—same business day** | Material risk requires a management, ethics, specialist, workload, or resource decision before ordinary review | Notify the responsible contact the same business day; record an interim plan and decision deadline |
| **Advice required before action** | The contemplated step should not proceed without specialist or supervisory review | Obtain and record review before the affected decision, filing, communication, or appearance |
| **System improvement** | No immediate client risk remains, but a process weakness requires correction | Submit through quality review without placing client information in an unrestricted record |

The escalation directory in Appendix F must identify primary and backup contacts
and an after-hours route. If a listed person is unavailable or does not respond
in time to protect the interest at stake, contact the next authorized person.
Do not allow uncertainty about the preferred recipient to delay an immediate
protective action or report.

The receiving supervisor must assess the client risk, identify a responsible
decision-maker, set a response time, and determine whether coverage,
reassignment, co-counsel, specialist assistance, investigation, continuance,
calendar relief, withdrawal, security containment, or another protective step
is required. A supervisor may not direct a lawyer to close an issue merely
because time or resources are unavailable.

### 13.4 Practice-critical and information-security incident response

Any person discovering a suspected practice-critical, confidentiality, or
security incident must:

1. take lawful steps within the person's authority to protect an imminent client
   right or safety interest;
2. notify the designated supervisor and incident lead immediately;
3. contain further disclosure or loss without altering or destroying relevant
   records;
4. preserve an accurate chronology, affected material, communications, and
   available system evidence in the approved restricted location; and
5. cooperate with the authorized assessment and remedial plan.

The incident lead, with ethics, privacy, security, management, coverage, or
other advice as appropriate, determines corrective action and any required
client, court, insurer, regulator, law-enforcement, or third-party communication.
Nothing in this process authorizes concealment or delay of a communication
required by law or professional duty. Personnel must not independently delete
records, alter the case file to obscure the event, admit liability on behalf of
the office, or make an external notification unless required by law or
authorized through the response process.

## 14. Calendaring, Case-Control, and Quality-Control Routine

### 14.1 Centralized deadline control

The approved centralized docketing system is the office system of record for
hearings and deadlines. A personal calendar, case-control page, email inbox,
court portal, prosecutor's notice, or handwritten note may supplement but must
not replace it.

Every hard deadline must include:

- the triggering event and source document;
- the governing authority and calculation method where calculation is required;
- date and time, including time zone when relevant;
- assigned lawyer, task owner, and backup owner;
- staged reminders sufficient for the work required; and
- an independent verification by a second authorized person.

The recipient of a court notice, changed hearing, offer, amended charge, custody
event, or related-jurisdiction update must route and enter it the same business
day unless earlier action is required. The assigned lawyer remains responsible
for confirming the legal effect and deadline. Any missed, uncertain, conflicting,
or disputed deadline is a required escalation event.

### 14.2 Control cadence

| Frequency / trigger | Minimum control |
|---|---|
| Daily | Reconcile imminent appearances, hard deadlines, expiring offers, custody matters, and unacknowledged critical communications |
| Weekly or supervisor-approved equivalent | Review each lawyer's trials, contested hearings, custody cases, material overdue work, open discovery, leave, and coverage capacity |
| Before planned leave | Assign and confirm coverage; update the control page, deadline inventory, and contact instructions |
| On unplanned absence | Supervisor identifies affected matters, activates coverage, and documents responsibility for immediate tasks |
| On receipt of material new information | Reopen affected controls and decision gates; update advice and deadlines as needed |
| At file transfer | Complete the ICR-11 handoff and obtain receiving-lawyer acceptance |

### 14.3 At assignment

- Open the case control page.
- Complete conflict review and urgent-risk screening.
- Calendar known hearings, deadlines, offer dates, and follow-up dates.
- Arrange prompt client contact and any accommodation.
- Begin the discovery ledger and preservation tasks.

### 14.4 Before each consequential client meeting or negotiation

- Refresh custody, conditions, offer, deadlines, and client objectives.
- Review the discovery ledger for referenced but unreceived or unreviewed items.
- Update the elements matrix and legal-issue worksheet.
- Identify material unknowns and explain how they affect advice.
- Prepare an individualized outcome comparison.

### 14.5 Before each contested hearing

- Complete the hearing plan.
- Confirm burden, authority, witnesses, exhibits, foundation, and logistics.
- Prepare the primary route, fallback route, and preservation steps.
- Reconfirm client communication and any decision allocated to the client.

### 14.6 At material change

A new report, participant, charge, offer, court date, custody event, or related
case triggers a targeted refresh of conflict, risk, discovery, merits, deadline,
and client-advice sections as applicable.

### 14.7 At closure or major milestone

- Confirm completion of remaining client communication and file obligations.
- Preserve the record according to office policy.
- Complete a de-identified post-matter review when the matter offers a reusable
  lesson.

## 16. Framework Performance Review

The purpose of measurement is to identify process failures and improve timely,
individualized representation—not to reward case speed or prescribe particular
outcomes. Quality review must respect privilege, confidentiality, defense
strategy, client autonomy, and counsel's professional independence.

### 16.1 Review program

The Managing Attorney must designate a qualified quality reviewer and approve a
written review schedule. The program must include:

- periodic review of a defined sample of open and recently closed matters;
- risk-triggered review after a missed or threatened deadline, significant
  complaint, security event, unstaffed matter, serious handoff failure, or other
  designated incident;
- heightened attention to custody cases, imminent trials, expiring offers,
  unreviewed material discovery, and matters requiring specialist advice;
- an opportunity for the responsible lawyer to supply context, correct the
  record, and respond to a proposed finding;
- a written corrective action, owner, due date, and verification step for each
  material control failure; and
- aggregate trend review for workload, staffing, training, technology,
  supervision, vendor, or policy causes.

Quality reviewers may access only the information reasonably necessary for an
authorized review and must use the restricted review location. A review record
must distinguish the client-file correction from any de-identified systemic
learning record. Quality material may not be distributed outside the authorized
review process without approval following legal, privilege, and confidentiality
review.

### 16.2 Process measures

Approved process measures may include:

| Measure | Review question |
|---|---|
| Timeliness of initial client contact | Were urgent custody and communication needs identified soon enough? |
| Conflict rechecks | Were new participants screened when they appeared? |
| Discovery reconciliation | Were referenced items accounted for before consequential advice? |
| Issue-to-action conversion | Did material findings receive an owner and deadline? |
| Consequence review | Were individualized consequences considered before a recommendation? |
| Hearing contingency preparation | Were foundation, fallback, and preservation routes prepared? |
| Feedback adoption | Did a reusable lesson result in a reviewed process change? |

Avoid using acquittal rates, plea rates, dismissal rates, or case-closure speed in
isolation as measures of representation quality. Outcomes depend on facts,
law, client choices, prosecutorial decisions, judicial rulings, and available
resources. Checklist-completion percentages also may not be used in isolation
to rank lawyers, infer competence, or obscure excessive workload. Any use of
handbook data for personnel action must comply with applicable employment,
collective-bargaining, privacy, and office policies and must account for the
accuracy and context of the data.

### 16.3 Closed-loop correction

| Finding | Client-specific protection | Systemic cause considered | Corrective action | Owner | Due date | Verification / closure |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | | | |

A material finding remains open until immediate client protection is complete,
the longer-term action is assigned, and an authorized reviewer verifies the
result. The office must prioritize restoration or preservation of client rights
over internal attribution of fault.
